The Civic AI Corps

A Regional Talent and Deployment Ecosystem for Southern Arizona

  

Prepared by: AI Trailblazers, in partnership with Pima County, Per Scholas, and Vantage West Credit Union

  

Reference: HP Digital Equity Accelerator

https://www.hp.com/us-en/sustainable-impact/digital-equity/digital-accelerator.html

  
  

The Problem

  

Southern Arizona has a growing gap between the organizations that need AI skills and the residents who could fill those roles. Community nonprofits, county offices, and small businesses are falling behind on automation, not because the technology is too expensive, but because they lack trained people to apply it.

  

At the same time, residents in unincorporated and rural parts of Pima County have fewer pathways into tech careers. One Stop workforce centers, digital navigators, and community colleges serve these populations, but none of them currently offer applied AI training. The result: organizations that need help can not find it, and residents who want to build new skills have nowhere to go.

  

No single organization in the region can close this gap alone. AITB reaches about 1,500 people directly. Per Scholas trains IT and cybersecurity professionals but does not yet have a Southern Arizona presence. Pima County connects thousands of households through the Office of Digital Inclusion, but does not run training programs. Vantage West serves a broad membership base, but its mission is financial wellness, not workforce development.

  

Together, the four partners reach over 25,000 residents across Southern Arizona. The Civic AI Corps is designed to turn that combined reach into real outcomes.

  
  

The Idea

  

We are not placing a few apprentices inside one institution. We are building a community talent network that helps public, nonprofit, and small business partners get more done with AI.

  

The model creates a new role: the Civic Automation Engineer. While Per Scholas trains "Defenders" (cybersecurity) and "Builders" (IT support), AITB trains "Automators." These learners do not just complete coursework. They apply AI and automation skills to real workflows inside community organizations across Southern Arizona.

  
  

How It Works

  

Learners move through a shared pathway with four stages:

  

1. Recruitment and community access through Pima County's digital inclusion network, One Stop centers, and partner referrals (including SBDC for small business candidates)

2. Technical foundation through Per Scholas, delivered through a parent/child satellite model in which the Southern Arizona satellite cohort mirrors the parent training program (for example, a Health IT parent cohort in Phoenix with a Tucson child cohort running the same program)

3. Paid applied AI apprenticeship through AITB, aligned to the Per Scholas pathway, where learners who have completed or are completing the parent/child technical training work on real automation projects inside community organizations

4. Financial wellness support through Vantage West, including one on one coaching to help participants build economic stability alongside new skills

  

During the paid apprenticeship, learners and mentors build secure "Copilots" and workflow automations for host organizations. This applied layer does not replace or alter the Per Scholas training program; it gives learners an applied project placement that supports their transition into paid employment. These projects reduce administrative overhead, increase service capacity, and give learners a portfolio of deployed work they can show future employers.

  

The deployment network is broad. Apprentices can support digital navigators at Pima County, financial coaches at Vantage West, staff at community nonprofits, small businesses connected through SBDC, and other anchor institutions across the region.

  
  

Partner Roles

  

Pima County (Office of Digital Inclusion, Michelle Simon)

Anchor implementation partner. Provides community access points through One Stop centers and digital navigator programs. Brings public sector use cases, resident pathways in unincorporated and rural areas, and the infrastructure to reach households that other partners can not access on their own.

  

Per Scholas (Sharise Erby)

Tuition free technical workforce training. Provides IT, cybersecurity, and other market-responsive technical coursework, learner support services, a parent/child satellite delivery model in which the Southern Arizona child cohort mirrors the Phoenix parent program, and a proven pathway for residents pivoting into technology careers. Per Scholas tracks paid job attainment for two years after graduation, including 60% of graduates employed in paid roles within 4 months and 80% within 12 months, giving the coalition a built in employment outcome framework.

  

AI Trailblazers (Aaron Eden)

Leads the Civic Automation Engineer curriculum and paid apprenticeship layer. Designs applied AI projects, matches learners with host organizations after or alongside the Per Scholas parent/child training pathway, mentors teams on real world automation work, and convenes the coalition locally.

  

Vantage West Credit Union (Jon Bruflat)

Workforce and economic development lens. Provides one on one financial coaching for participants, community credibility, sponsorship, and promotional reach across its membership base. Connects the program to broader economic mobility outcomes.

  
  

Why This Coalition Works

  

Each partner fills a gap the others can not cover:

  

Pima County has the reach into underserved communities but does not run training programs. Per Scholas has the training model but no Southern Arizona footprint. AITB has the AI curriculum and paid apprenticeship framework but a small direct audience. Vantage West has the community trust and financial coaching capability but is not a workforce trainer.

  

Together, the four organizations can recruit from a broader population, train in more locations, deploy apprentices into more host organizations, and track outcomes more rigorously than any one of them could alone.

  

This model also solves the indirect reach challenge. AITB directly serves about 1,500 people. Through the coalition, the program touches Pima County's digital inclusion households, Per Scholas's learner pipeline, Vantage West's membership network, and SBDC's small business community. Combined, the coalition's indirect reach exceeds 25,000 residents across Southern Arizona.

  
  

Target Outcomes (Year 1)

  

Learners trained: 30 Civic Automation Engineers complete the full pathway

Host organizations served: 15 community organizations receive deployed automation projects

Job attainment: 60% of graduates employed in paid roles within 4 months and 80% within 12 months, tracked by Per Scholas

Financial wellness: 100% of participants receive Vantage West financial coaching

Community reach: 25,000+ residents reached through combined partner networks

Projects deployed: 30+ automation tools or Copilots built and delivered to host organizations

  
  

Implementation Timeline

  

Phase 1 (Months 1 to 3): Foundation

Formalize partner agreements. Design the Civic Automation Engineer curriculum. Identify first cohort of host organizations. Set up the Per Scholas parent/child satellite model for Southern Arizona, ensuring the Tucson child cohort mirrors the Phoenix parent program. Build recruitment pipeline through Pima County and SBDC.

  

Phase 2 (Months 4 to 6): First Cohort

Recruit and enroll 15 learners. Begin Per Scholas technical training through the aligned parent/child model. Launch Vantage West financial coaching. Match learners with host organizations for paid AITB apprenticeship placements that complement the Per Scholas pathway.

  

Phase 3 (Months 7 to 12): Deployment and Scale

Paid apprentices deploy automation projects at host organizations. Recruit second cohort of 15 learners. Document outcomes and case studies. Begin planning for Year 2 expansion.

  
  

Sustainability

  

The Civic AI Corps is designed to sustain itself beyond initial grant funding in three ways:

  

1. Host organizations that benefit from paid apprentice work become future sponsors and employers, creating a self reinforcing cycle of demand.

2. Per Scholas's existing national infrastructure reduces the cost of maintaining Southern Arizona operations once the parent/child satellite model is established.

3. Vantage West and Pima County bring institutional staying power. Their participation is not grant dependent. It aligns with their existing missions in financial wellness and digital inclusion.
